Case Study

Evidence-Based Course Review for Access, Clarity, and Learner Success

Executive Summary

This project is a self-paced course for faculty, instructional designers, and academic partners in higher education. It teaches a repeatable process for reviewing course artifacts, interpreting evidence across multiple dimensions, and prioritizing the changes that most improve clarity, access, and learner support. A consistent module and milestone structure helps make instructional judgment teachable.

Although the course has not yet run with learners, it is designed to improve consistency in institutional course-review practices, make reviewer reasoning more visible, and support clearer, more defensible faculty-facing recommendations.

Opening Positioning Statement

I design scalable learning systems that help educators make course improvement work clear, structured, and usable.

This project is a self-paced course for faculty, instructional designers, and academic partners. It teaches an evidence-based process for reviewing course design, identifying barriers, evaluating priorities, and writing practical, faculty-facing recommendations.

I designed the course as a complete learning system rather than a set of content pages. A consistent module structure, milestone outputs, and embedded supports guide learners from noticing course features to developing evidence-based recommendations.

The result is a cohesive experience in which the architecture, milestone logic, supports, and final performance all reinforce the same reasoning process.

Project Snapshot

Project: Evidence-Based Course Review for Access, Clarity, and Learner Success

Audience: Faculty, instructional designers, and academic partners in higher education who review and improve online courses.

Format: Self-paced online course, five modules plus a final performance task.

Core Focus: Teaches an evidence-based process for reviewing course design, identifying meaningful barriers, prioritizing improvements, and writing faculty-facing recommendations.

Tool: Articulate Rise

Timeline: One week, solo design

AI Integration: AI-supported workflows used for drafting, iteration, and refinement of instructional material

Final Performance: A multi-artifact course review that culminates in a prioritization rationale, a faculty improvement brief, and a short review integrity reflection.

My Role: End-to-end learning experience design, including architecture, writing, scaffolding, accessibility and UDL integration, interaction design, visual system, and portfolio deployment.

What This Project Demonstrates

Teaches a judgment skill that many courses leave implicit. Course review is often treated like a checklist task.

This course makes the reasoning behind review decisions visible, teachable, and transferable without instructor mediation.

Backward design applied at the system level, not just the lesson level.

Every module, milestone, and support was built in service of a single authentic performance task defined before any content was written.

The Challenge

Learners needed to distinguish evidence from assumption, evaluate course quality across five dimensions, prioritize improvements using visible tradeoffs, and write supportive faculty-facing recommendations without instructor mediation.

At the same time, the course needed to reduce ambiguity, hidden expectations, and workflow confusion while preserving authentic analytical work and maintaining several non-negotiables: multi-artifact evidence-based review, multidimensional evaluation, prioritization of meaningful improvements, rigor-preserving recommendations, and a focus on course design rather than learner-deficit assumptions.

Design Strategy

I structured the course around a visible reasoning sequence that guides learners from initial observation to faculty-facing recommendation. This sequence anchors the learning experience and prevents learners from moving too quickly from impression to judgment.

Observation → Evidence → Interpretation → Prioritization → Recommendation

The sequence also reflects the logic of backward design: the final performance task, a faculty-facing recommendation grounded in evidence and prioritization, was defined first, and every module, milestone, and support was built in service of that outcome. Starting from the desired performance kept the design anchored to authentic transfer rather than content coverage. This decision ensured that every module, milestone, and support aligned with the final faculty-facing task.

Learning Architecture

I organized the course as a five-module progression that mirrors the actual flow of course review work. Each module aligns to one stage of the reasoning sequence, moving learners from establishing a review stance to gathering evidence, evaluating course quality, prioritizing improvements, and writing an independent faculty-facing recommendation. This structure treats course review as a staged process rather than a single judgment made at the end.

A key design decision was the use of milestone outputs. Each module produces a working artifact, such as review notes, an evidence log, evaluation notes, or a prioritization rationale, that feeds directly into the next stage. This carry-forward logic makes the reasoning trail visible across the course and helps learners build the final recommendation incrementally rather than generating it all at once.

I also used a consistent module pattern to reduce navigation burden in a self-paced environment. Learners encounter the same visible flow across modules: purpose, reading and tool use, checkpoint task, quality check, and carry-forward guidance. Early modules provide fuller models and stronger scaffolding, then support gradually fades as learners move toward independent judgment.

Scaffolding, Accessibility, and Inclusion

I built support directly into the workflow of the course rather than treating it as an added layer. Milestone outputs carry reasoning forward from module to module, while evidence tools, guided comparisons, prioritization supports, and quality checks help learners manage the most demanding parts of the review process. These supports make the work more usable without replacing the core tasks of evidence-based review, multidimensional evaluation, prioritization, and recommendation writing.

Access is also supported through the course’s visible structure. Repeated section layouts, stable terminology, visible success criteria, reading-purpose cues, and point-of-use prompts help learners understand how the course works and what strong performance looks like. Guided comparisons and quality checks are placed at high-demand points so learners can test their thinking before moving forward.

Support fades as learners progress. Early modules include fuller models, stronger prompting, and more guided checks. Later modules retain milestone outputs, visible criteria, and self-review tools, but learners take greater responsibility for generating judgments, defending tradeoffs, and completing an independent review. This progression keeps the final task challenging while helping learners arrive prepared.

Key Design Decisions

1. I standardized the module architecture.

Every module follows the same visible pattern: purpose, reading and tool use, checkpoint task, quality check, and carry-forward guidance. This consistency reduces navigation burden in a self-paced environment so learners can keep their attention on the reasoning work rather than relearning how the course operates at each stage.

2. I made the reasoning chain explicit.

I made the Observation → Evidence → Interpretation → Prioritization → Recommendation sequence visible in module framing, task prompts, and success criteria. This helps learners see what kind of thinking each module requires and gives them a clear structure to use beyond the course.

3. I embedded supports at the point of use.

Rather than placing help in optional extras or side materials, I integrated supports directly into the workflow through evidence tools, guided comparisons, prioritization prompts, writing supports, and quality checks. Placement matters: support offered at the moment of highest cognitive demand is more useful and more likely to be used than support placed before or after the hard work.

4. I designed the final task to evaluate instructional judgment rather than polished writing alone.

Learners use their review work to develop a faculty-facing recommendation. The task emphasizes clear reasoning and a defensible improvement choice.

Authentic Performance Design

I designed the culminating task to reflect the kind of work educators and instructional support professionals do in real course improvement contexts. Instead of ending with a quiz or a summary, the course asks learners to complete a multi-artifact review, examine course design across five dimensions, prioritize meaningful improvements, and write a concise faculty-facing recommendation. This keeps the final performance grounded in authentic practice rather than simulated completion.

The performance task is also built to make judgment visible. Learners do not submit a recommendation alone. They complete a diagnostic evidence log, an improvement prioritization rationale, a faculty improvement brief, and a short review integrity reflection. Together, these components show how the learner moved from evidence to evaluation to prioritization to recommendation, which protects the integrity of the final task and keeps the course aligned to its core construct.

The result is a performance design that supports transfer. Learners study a parallel exemplar for structure, then complete their own independent review on a different case. That distinction matters. It allows the course to model strong practice while still requiring fresh analysis, tradeoff reasoning, and faculty-facing communication. In that way, the final task reflects authentic faculty development practice and reinforces the course’s larger goal: helping educators make better course design decisions through visible, evidence-based judgment.

Outcome and Reflection

This project turns course review into a structured, repeatable process that educators can use independently in self-paced environments.

The finished course teaches learners how to move from observable evidence to multidimensional evaluation, prioritization, and faculty-facing recommendation writing. It demonstrates how complex instructional judgment can be made teachable online without instructor mediation.

This course is currently in portfolio deployment. No learner cohort has completed it yet. Outcome targets include reviewers completing the five-stage reasoning sequence without instructor support, milestone artifacts demonstrating evidence-to-recommendation logic, and faculty-facing briefs that prioritize improvements rather than list observations. These targets were embedded into the performance design from the start and will anchor future evaluation if the course is deployed.

For me, the strongest part of this project is how fully the course functions as a system. The architecture, milestone logic, point-of-use supports, accessibility decisions, and final performance task all reinforce the same reasoning process. That coherence helps learners understand not only what to do, but how to think through the work in a disciplined and transferable way.

If I were refining the course further, I would continue strengthening visual signaling and smoothing a few transitions between comparison, interpretation, and final writing. The core strategy is strong, but experience design can always become sharper through iteration. That mindset is central to how I work: build for clarity, look for friction, and improve the system without losing sight of the learning.
