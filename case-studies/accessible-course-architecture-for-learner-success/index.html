Case Study

Accessible Course Architecture for Learner Access, Navigation, and Progress

View Course Back to Portfolio

Executive Summary

This project is a self-paced course for faculty and instructional designers in higher education. It helps participants examine how course architecture shapes the learner experience through organization, navigation, pacing, and instructional routines, then use that analysis to plan targeted structural improvements.

The course is built around a simple but consequential idea: learners experience a course through the paths it creates. Participants study how modules, pages, tasks, and support systems work together, identify where structure creates unnecessary effort, and develop a focused course-level response that improves clarity and usability while preserving academic rigor.

Although the course has not yet run with a learner cohort, the design has been reviewed against established course quality frameworks, including UDL guidelines and backward design principles, and the course itself models the analytical process it teaches. It is designed to surface hidden design barriers and support more coherent, learner-centered course environments in self-paced higher education contexts.

Opening Positioning Statement

Most course design attention goes to content. I focus on the paths learners have to move through to reach it.

In this project, I designed a self-paced course for faculty and instructional designers in higher education. It teaches participants how to examine course architecture through a learner-centered lens, identify barriers in module organization, navigation pathways, pacing, and instructional routines, and plan improvements that strengthen access and progress.

I designed the course as an integrated learning system rather than a sequence of standalone lessons. Its consistent module structure, milestone-based outputs, and guided reading protocols move participants through a clear progression: noticing learner-facing design features, diagnosing points of friction, and developing a focused improvement plan.

The result is a course that treats architecture as part of learning, not just as the frame around it. Its design helps educators make course pathways more legible, support more visible, and participation easier to sustain, while preserving rigor.

What This Project Demonstrates

This project demonstrates four capabilities that are underrepresented in instructional design portfolios.

Designs for learner movement, not just content delivery. This project shows my ability to design for the conditions learners move through: how they orient, navigate, find support, and sustain progress across a course. Structure is treated as an instructional variable, not a neutral frame.

Turns hidden structural barriers into design decisions. Rather than treating confusion or disengagement as learner problems, the course reframes them as design conditions that can be analyzed, prioritized, and improved. That shift in framing is itself one of the things participants learn.

Builds coherent learning systems. The project demonstrates my ability to align course structure, scaffolds, guided reading protocols, milestone logic, and final performance so that each part of the experience supports the same learner-facing goal.

Balances usability with academic rigor. This work reflects my approach to accessible learning design: reducing unnecessary effort, strengthening clarity, and preserving the level and purpose of the academic work.

The Challenge

I created this project as part of my instructional design portfolio to demonstrate a dimension of learning experience design that is often underrepresented: how course architecture shapes learner access, navigation, and progress. Many portfolio pieces highlight content development or lesson sequencing. Fewer show how learners actually move through a course and where structure itself can create unnecessary barriers.

In many courses, learners experience structure as friction. Required tasks are hard to locate, module patterns shift from week to week, pacing is uneven, and support is not always visible when it is needed. What can look like disengagement or confusion is often a problem of course architecture rather than learner effort.

This project needed to help faculty and instructional designers analyze those conditions through a learner-centered lens. Participants had to examine module organization, navigation pathways, pacing, and instructional routines using observable evidence, then identify which points of friction most affected learner access, clarity, and progress.

The course also had to support that work in a self-paced format, without instructor mediation. That meant turning architectural analysis into a process learners could follow, practice, and apply independently while preserving the complexity of the task. The solution could not collapse course improvement into a checklist of fixes. It needed to support careful judgment, meaningful prioritization, and targeted design changes that reduce friction without reducing rigor.

Design Strategy

I structured the course around a visible process for analyzing course architecture and translating that analysis into focused design decisions. The sequence slows judgment down and keeps participants close to what learners actually encounter as they move through a course.

Stance → Evidence → Evaluation → Prioritization → Architecture Plan

I built that sequence backward from the final performance. The course architecture plan was defined first, then each module, milestone, and support was designed to prepare learners for that task. This kept the experience grounded in the design work itself and ensured that every part of the course contributed to the same outcome.

A central strategic decision was to treat course structure as part of the learner experience itself. Participants are asked to attend to what learners can see, click, locate, follow, and infer across modules, pages, and tasks. This keeps the work grounded in visible structural conditions and shifts analysis away from learner-deficit interpretations.

I also designed the course to make improvement work focused and usable. Rather than asking participants to fix every issue they notice, the course teaches them to identify the design barriers that most affect access, navigation, clarity, and progress, then plan targeted changes that reduce friction while preserving academic expectations.

Learning Architecture

I organized the course as a five-module progression that mirrors the real flow of course architecture analysis and improvement. Each module aligns to a distinct stage in that process, moving participants from establishing an analysis stance to documenting structural evidence, evaluating architectural issues, prioritizing improvements, and building a targeted architecture response.

A key design decision was the use of milestone outputs. Each module produces a concrete artifact: stance notes, analysis log entries, evaluation notes, or prioritization rationale. Each artifact feeds directly into the next stage of the course. This carry-forward logic makes participants’ reasoning visible over time and helps them build the final architecture plan step by step rather than all at once.

I also used a consistent module pattern across all 32 lessons to reduce navigation burden in a self-paced environment. Participants encounter the same visible flow in every module: framing, guided reading, guided analysis, checkpoint, working output, and wrap-up. That predictability keeps attention on the design work itself and models the kind of clear, coherent structure the course is asking participants to create.

Each module wrap-up closes the loop explicitly, summarizing what participants now have and pointing forward to how that artifact will be used in the next stage. This prevents the working outputs from feeling like disconnected exercises and maintains a sense of cumulative progress across the course.

Key Decisions

Several decisions shaped how this course works as a learning system. Each one responds to a specific design problem and supports the larger goal of making course architecture visible, analyzable, and improvable.

1. I anchored the analysis in learner-facing structure.

The course asks participants to focus on what learners can see, click, locate, follow, and infer across modules, pages, and tasks. This keeps the analysis grounded in observable course conditions and shifts attention away from learner-deficit explanations.

2. I used milestone outputs to carry reasoning forward.

Each module produces a concrete artifact that feeds directly into the next stage. This makes the reasoning process visible and helps participants build a complex final task step by step rather than all at once. The reasoning chain, structural feature, verifiable evidence, likely learner demand, later evaluation, is surfaced explicitly at each stage so participants can trace their own thinking across modules.

3. I standardized the module pattern.

Every module follows the same visible flow: framing, guided reading, guided analysis, checkpoint, working output, and wrap-up. In a self-paced course without instructor mediation, that predictability reduces navigation burden and keeps attention on the analytical work rather than on how the course is organized.

4. I embedded support where judgment gets harder.

Checkpoints, guided comparisons, and quality checks appear where participants need to test evidence, interpret structural issues, and make prioritization decisions. Each checkpoint uses the same format: choose the stronger statement from two options, then explain why. This keeps support inside the workflow of the course rather than separating help from the work itself.

5. I bounded the improvement work.

The course does not ask participants to redesign an entire course. It teaches them to identify the structural issues that most affect access, navigation, clarity, and progress, then plan targeted changes that improve usability while maintaining academic expectations.

This model reinforces the course’s emphasis on cumulative reasoning by showing how each stage produces a concrete artifact that supports the next.

Scaffolding, Accessibility, and Inclusion

I built support directly into the structure of the course rather than treating it as an added layer. Milestone outputs carry reasoning forward from module to module, while guided prompts, checkpoints, and quality checks help participants manage the most demanding parts of evidence gathering, evaluation, and planning.

Access is supported through the course’s visible structure. Repeated module patterns, stable terminology, consistent placement of instructions and support, and clear signaling of required versus optional work reduce the need for inference and allow participants to move through the course with less unnecessary effort.

Support fades over time. Early modules include more structured prompts, examples, and guided checks. Later modules retain milestone outputs and visible criteria, but participants take greater responsibility for generating analysis, defending tradeoffs, and completing a focused course architecture plan on their own. This progression preserves the complexity of the task while making success more achievable.

The optional lens structure adds a second layer of access without restructuring the required path. Participants who want additional conceptual grounding or examples can use the optional readings and their guided questions without the course implying they need remediation.

Authentic Performance Design

The course also makes judgment visible through checkpoint interactions that ask participants to compare options, justify choices, and interpret structural evidence before moving forward.

The culminating task asks participants to develop a focused, evidence-grounded course architecture plan, not a broad redesign, and not a quiz. It reflects the bounded, judgment-based work that faculty and instructional designers actually do when they improve a course: identify the structural issues that matter most, explain why they matter, and propose targeted changes that strengthen the learning environment while preserving academic expectations.

What makes the task transferable is not the plan itself but the process it requires participants to complete. By the time they draft the final plan, participants have moved through five staged working outputs: stance notes, analysis log entries, evaluation notes, prioritization rationale, and architecture planning notes. That visible chain of reasoning is the portable output. It gives participants a structured analytical process they can apply to any course, not just the one they used during the course.

Outcome and Reflection

This project demonstrates how strongly course architecture shapes the conditions for learning. The finished course helps participants see how structure influences orientation, navigation, support, and progress, then translate those insights into targeted improvements that make course environments more coherent and usable.

It also shows that many barriers in higher education are not located in the content alone, but in the way learners are expected to move through that content. By centering visible design features and learner-facing conditions, the course helps educators identify where hidden effort accumulates and where structural changes can make participation easier to sustain.

The course is currently in portfolio deployment and has not yet run with a live cohort. Readiness indicators include internal alignment with CAST UDL guidelines and backward design principles, consistent execution of the module pattern across all 32 lessons, and milestone scaffolding that builds toward a clearly defined culminating performance. The next evaluation priority is live pilot data to confirm participant ability to identify architectural obstacles, trace their effects on learner movement, and develop focused improvement plans.

For me, this project extends my practice from evaluating learning experiences to shaping the environments that hold them. It reflects how I approach instructional design at its strongest: as the design of pathways, supports, and structures that help learners stay oriented and keep moving.

If I were refining the course further, I would continue strengthening visual signaling, clarifying key pathways across modules, and adding a worked example of a completed architecture plan. The core design is strong. The next round of iteration would make the experience even more coherent, navigable, and precise.
